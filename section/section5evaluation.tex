\section{Experiments}\label{section:evaluation}

\subsection{Setup}


\textbf{Basic Settings.} We use Llama-2-7B-Chat~\cite{hu2023llama} as the base model. For all LoRA training, we adopt the weclone pipeline~\cite{weclone} with rank $r=8$, alpha $\alpha=16$, and dropout $0.1$ applied to query, key, value, and output projection matrices. Training uses 4$\times$ NVIDIA A100-80GB GPUs with DeepSpeed ZeRO-2 and BF16 mixed precision; the effective batch size is 32 with a learning rate of $2\times10^{-4}$ under cosine scheduling. For inference, we apply 4-bit NF4 quantization and FlashAttention-2~\cite{dao2023flashattention} for efficient attention computation. Unless otherwise specified, the full system (WebDoctor) uses per-physician LoRA-Single adapters with identity-based LAG routing and TIES-MERGING at trim fraction $\tau=0.20$.

\textbf{Datasets.} We evaluate on the Internet Hospital Doctor-Patient Dialogue (IHDPD) dataset described in Section~4. The dataset spans 15 clinical departments and $K=120$ physicians (each with $\ge50$ consultation records), comprising 30,146 de-identified multi-turn dialogues in total. Each physician's data is split into training (70\%), validation (15\%), and test (15\%) sets. Table~\ref{tab:dataset_len} summarizes the dataset statistics, including per-department physician counts and average dialogue length.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{High-resource departments}: Internal Medicine (24 physicians, 4,821 dialogues), Cardiology (14 physicians, 2,953 dialogues), Dermatology (11 physicians, 2,374 dialogues).
    \item \textbf{Medium-resource departments}: Pediatrics (10 physicians, 2,018 dialogues), Obstetrics \& Gynecology (9 physicians, 1,826 dialogues), Orthopedics and Neurology (8 physicians each, 1,597 and 1,623 dialogues).
    \item \textbf{Low-resource departments}: Psychiatry (5 physicians, 912 dialogues), Ophthalmology (5 physicians, 1,041 dialogues), Otolaryngology and Urology (4 physicians each), Respiratory Medicine (3 physicians), General Surgery (2 physicians).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Baselines.}
We compare against the following personalization and adapter-merging baselines:
(1)~\textbf{Base Model}: Llama-2-7B-Chat with a physician-identifying prompt prefix but without any fine-tuning or adaptation;
(2)~\textbf{Shared LoRA}: a single LoRA adapter trained on the aggregated dialogues of all $K$ physicians, capturing overall clinical style without physician-specific differentiation;
(3)~\textbf{LoRA-Merge-Avg (Simple Averaging)}: element-wise averaging of all $K$ individual LoRA matrices, a straightforward but interference-prone fusion baseline;
(4)~\textbf{LoRA-Merge-TA (Task Arithmetic)}~\cite{ilharco2023editing}: task-vector arithmetic applied to LoRA deltas with a scaling coefficient tuned on the validation set;
(5)~\textbf{LoRA-Merge-TIES}: TIES-MERGING~\cite{yadav2024ties} fusion without the LAG routing component, using a shared prompt prefix for physician conditioning.
We also report \textbf{LoRA-Single}, the individual per-physician LoRA adapter loaded on demand, as the personalization upper bound.
For routing evaluation, we additionally compare identity-based LAG routing against query-based LAG routing (with top-$m=1$ retrieval via FAISS~\cite{lag2024}) and a random routing baseline.


\textbf{Performance Metrics.}
We evaluate along three axes:
(1)~Automatic metrics: BLEU-4~\cite{papineni2002bleu}, ROUGE-L~\cite{lin2004rouge}, and BERTScore~\cite{zhang2020bertscore} with respect to each physician's held-out gold responses. We also report validation-set perplexity (PPL) as a measure of adaptation quality.
(2)~Human evaluation: three board-certified physicians rate 100 randomly sampled test queries each on 5-point Likert scales across three dimensions---\emph{Personalization} (stylistic and terminological fidelity to the target physician), \emph{Clinical Accuracy} (medical correctness and appropriateness), and \emph{Fluency} (grammaticality and naturalness)---plus blind A/B preference judgments. Inter-rater agreement reaches Fleiss' $\kappa=0.72$ for personalization and $\kappa=0.68$ for clinical accuracy (substantial agreement).
(3)~Deployment efficiency: per-adapter storage footprint (MB), adapter loading latency (ms), and end-to-end inference latency (seconds per response). For merged-adapter deployments, we additionally report the \emph{Retention Rate}---the ratio of the merged adapter's BLEU-4 to that of the individual LoRA-Single adapter, expressed as a percentage.

\subsection{Results}

\subsubsection{Automatic Evaluation}~\\
Table~\ref{tab:accuracy} reports the automatic evaluation results across all methods.
The Base Model achieves only BLEU-4 5.1, confirming that zero-shot prompting, even with physician-identifying prefixes, cannot produce physician-specific outputs.
Shared LoRA improves to BLEU-4 7.8, demonstrating that general clinical adaptation is beneficial, but the lack of per-physician differentiation leaves a substantial gap relative to individual adaptation.

Among the merging baselines, simple averaging (LoRA-Merge-Avg) performs worst, with a BLEU-4 of 5.8 and a retention rate of only 55.8\%, indicating severe parameter interference when naively averaging $K=120$ adapters.
Task arithmetic (LoRA-Merge-TA) improves retention to 83.7\% (BLEU-4 8.7), but still loses over 16\% of individual performance.
TIES-MERGING achieves the strongest fusion result, with a retention rate of 97.1\% (BLEU-4 10.1), nearly matching the individual LoRA-Single gold standard (BLEU-4 10.4, PPL 17.6).
The full WebDoctor system, which combines TIES-MERGING with identity-based LAG routing and physician-specific prompt prefixes, matches the LoRA-Single upper bound across all metrics (BLEU-4 10.4, ROUGE-L 20.8, BERTScore 0.73, PPL 17.6), demonstrating that the merged deployment mode introduces no measurable personalization degradation.

\subsubsection{Human Evaluation}~\\
Table~\ref{tab:ttft} presents the human evaluation results.
The Base Model receives a personalization rating of only 2.1/5.0 and a preference share of 5\%, corroborating the automatic findings: physicians do not perceive the generic outputs as matching their communication style.
Shared LoRA achieves moderate improvement (personalization 2.8, preference 12\%), but still falls well below acceptable levels for clinical deployment.
The LoRA-Merge-TIES variant (personalization 4.0, clinical accuracy 4.2, preference 28\%) demonstrates that merged-adapter deployment can achieve high-quality personalization, though individual adapters retain a marginal lead.
The full WebDoctor system achieves the highest ratings across all dimensions (personalization 4.2, clinical accuracy 4.3, fluency 4.2, preference 42\%), with physicians preferring its outputs in nearly half of all blind A/B comparisons.
Notably, clinical accuracy is consistently high across all adapted methods (4.0--4.3), confirming that personalization does not compromise medical correctness; the primary differentiation occurs along the personalization dimension, where physician-specific adaptation yields the largest gains.

\subsubsection{LAG Routing Accuracy}~\\
We evaluate the LAG router's query-based retrieval accuracy on the test set.
When the target physician is among the top-$m$ candidates, identity-based routing is triggered; otherwise, the query-based route is taken.
With $m=1$, the router achieves a top-1 accuracy of 82.4\%, meaning it correctly identifies the target physician from the patient query alone in over four-fifths of cases.
Accuracy rises to 94.7\% at top-3 and 98.1\% at top-5, indicating that even when the exact physician is not the top-ranked candidate, the correct adapter is reliably retrieved within a small candidate set.
Random routing (uniform selection among $K=120$ physicians) achieves only 0.8\% top-1 accuracy, confirming that the LAG embeddings encode meaningful physician-style discriminative information.
The routing overhead is negligible (4.8\,ms per query), well within the real-time latency budget of internet hospital workflows ($<5$\,ms target).


\subsection{Component Validation and Ablation Study}

\subsubsection{LoRA Rank and Data Efficiency}~\\
We vary the LoRA rank $r \in \{4, 8, 16, 32\}$ to assess the impact of adapter capacity on personalization quality.
Table~\ref{tab:ablation} (top block) shows that $r=8$ offers the best accuracy-efficiency trade-off: $r=4$ achieves BLEU-4 9.5 with a 4.2\,MB adapter footprint, while $r=8$ reaches BLEU-4 10.4 with 6.8\,MB, and $r=16$ yields only a marginal gain (BLEU-4 10.5) at nearly double the storage cost (13.1\,MB).
$r=32$ provides no further improvement (BLEU-4 10.5), suggesting that the personalization signal saturates at moderate rank.
This is consistent with the limited per-physician training data (median 186 dialogues): higher-rank adapters overfit without additional regularization.

We further examine the relationship between per-physician training set size and BLEU-4.
Physicians in the top quartile by dialogue count ($\ge 287$ dialogues, mean BLEU-4 11.2) outperform those in the bottom quartile ($\le 98$ dialogues, mean BLEU-4 9.1), confirming that personalization quality benefits from larger individual corpora.
However, even the bottom-quartile physicians achieve a BLEU-4 more than double that of the Base Model (5.1), demonstrating the data efficiency of LoRA-based adaptation.

\subsubsection{TIES-MERGING Performance Preservation}~\\
Table~\ref{tab:ties_retention} reports the retention rate of TIES-MERGING as a function of the trim fraction $\tau$ and the number of merged adapters $K$.
At the optimal $\tau=0.20$ and the full physician pool ($K=120$), TIES-MERGING retains 97.1\% of individual adapter performance (BLEU-4 10.1 vs.\ 10.4).
Retention is robust across a range of $\tau$ values: $\tau=0.15$ achieves 94.2\%, $\tau=0.25$ achieves 95.2\%, and only at extremes ($\tau\le0.10$ or $\tau\ge0.40$) does retention drop below 90\%.
Scaling $K$ from 20 to 120 physicians, the retention rate degrades gracefully from 98.6\% to 97.1\%, indicating that the ELECT SIGN step effectively resolves sign conflicts even as the adapter pool grows.
In contrast, simple averaging degrades sharply from 87.3\% at $K=20$ to 55.8\% at $K=120$, confirming that interference accumulates multiplicatively without explicit sign resolution.

\textbf{Deployment Efficiency.} Each individual LoRA adapter requires 6.8\,MB of storage and approximately 120\,ms to load from disk.
For $K=120$ physicians, per-adapter serving would require 816\,MB of aggregate storage and, in the worst case, 120\,ms of loading latency per distinct physician request.
The merged LoRA-Merge-TIES adapter occupies 6.8\,MB (equivalent to a single adapter) and is loaded once at server startup, eliminating per-request adapter switching overhead.
End-to-end inference latency with the merged adapter is 1.8\,s per response on a single A100 GPU, identical to the individual adapter case, confirming that TIES-MERGING introduces no computational overhead at inference time.

\subsubsection{Ablation Study}~\\
We ablate the key design choices of WebDoctor, reporting BLEU-4 and human-evaluated personalization scores (5-point scale).
Table~\ref{tab:ablation} (bottom block) presents the results.
Removing the LAG router and relying solely on a generic prompt prefix (``w/o LAG'') causes a moderate drop (BLEU-4 10.2, personalization 4.0), confirming that explicit physician identity conditioning is important for stylistic fidelity.
Disabling TIES-MERGING and serving individual adapters (``w/o Merge'') preserves full personalization quality (BLEU-4 10.4, personalization 4.2) but at the cost of $K$-fold storage and loading overhead, as discussed above.
Removing both LAG and TIES-MERGING (i.e., Shared LoRA) yields the largest degradation (BLEU-4 7.8, personalization 2.8), confirming that both components contribute independently to personalization quality.
Varying the TIES trim fraction confirms the optimal $\tau=0.20$: $\tau=0.10$ is overly aggressive (BLEU-4 9.3, retention 89.4\%), discarding useful parameters, while $\tau=0.30$ retains more parameters but introduces noise, reducing BLEU-4 slightly (9.5).
