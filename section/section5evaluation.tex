\section{Experiments}\label{section:evaluation}

\subsection{Setup}


\textbf{Basic Settings.} We evaluate on three base models spanning distinct capability tiers: LLaMA-3.2-1B-Instruct, LLaMA-3.2-3B-Instruct~\cite{grattafioriLlama3Herd2024}, and Qwen-2.5-7B-Instruct~\cite{qwenQwen25TechnicalReport2025}. For all LoRA training, we adopt Unsloth~\cite{unsloth2024}~\cite{weclone} for LoRA fine-tuning with rank $r=8$, alpha $\alpha=16$, and dropout $0.1$ applied to query, key, value, and output projection matrices. Training uses 8$\times$ NVIDIA H20-3e (96GB) GPUs with DeepSpeed ZeRO-2 and BF16 mixed precision; the effective batch size is 32 with a learning rate of $2\times10^{-4}$ under cosine scheduling. For inference, we apply 4-bit NF4 quantization (1B/3B) or 8-bit quantization (7B) and FlashAttention-2~\cite{dao2023flashattention} for efficient attention computation. Unless otherwise specified, the full system (WebDoctor) uses per-physician LoRA-Single adapters with identity-based Doctor-Adaptive Routing (DAR) and Sign-Consistent Fusion (SCF) at trim fraction $\tau=0.20$.

\textbf{Datasets.} We evaluate on the Internet Hospital Doctor-Patient Dialogue (IHDPD) dataset described in Section~4. The dataset spans 15 clinical departments and $K=120$ physicians (each with $\ge50$ consultation records), comprising 30,146 de-identified multi-turn dialogues in total. Each physician's data is split into training (70\%), validation (15\%), and test (15\%) sets. Table~\ref{tab:dataset_len} summarizes the dataset statistics, including per-department physician counts and average dialogue length.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{High-resource departments}: Internal Medicine (24 physicians, 4,821 dialogues), Cardiology (14 physicians, 2,953 dialogues), Dermatology (11 physicians, 2,374 dialogues).
    \item \textbf{Medium-resource departments}: Pediatrics (10 physicians, 2,018 dialogues), Obstetrics \& Gynecology (9 physicians, 1,826 dialogues), Orthopedics and Neurology (8 physicians each, 1,597 and 1,623 dialogues).
    \item \textbf{Low-resource departments}: Psychiatry (5 physicians, 912 dialogues), Ophthalmology (5 physicians, 1,041 dialogues), Otolaryngology and Urology (4 physicians each), Respiratory Medicine (3 physicians), General Surgery (2 physicians).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Baselines.}
We compare against the following personalization and adapter-merging baselines:
(1)~\textbf{Base Model}: the unadapted base model with a physician-identifying prompt prefix but without any fine-tuning;
(2)~\textbf{Shared LoRA}: a single LoRA adapter trained on the aggregated dialogues of all $K$ physicians, capturing overall clinical style without physician-specific differentiation;
(3)~\textbf{LoRA-Merge-Avg}: element-wise averaging of all $K$ individual LoRA matrices, a straightforward but interference-prone fusion baseline;
(4)~\textbf{LoRA-Merge-TA (Task Arithmetic)}~\cite{ilharco2023editing}: task-vector arithmetic applied to LoRA deltas with a scaling coefficient tuned on the validation set;
(5)~\textbf{LoRA-Merge-TIES}~\cite{yadav2024ties}: the original TIES-MERGING fusion algorithm applied without our DAR routing component, using a shared prompt prefix for physician conditioning.
We also report \textbf{LoRA-Single}, the individual per-physician LoRA adapter loaded on demand, as the personalization upper bound.
For routing evaluation, we additionally compare identity-based DAR against query-based DAR (with top-$m=1$ retrieval via FAISS~\cite{johnson2019faiss}) and a random routing baseline.


\textbf{Performance Metrics.}
We evaluate along three axes:
(1)~Automatic metrics: BLEU-4~\cite{papineni2002bleu}, ROUGE-L~\cite{lin2004rouge}, and BERTScore~\cite{zhang2020bertscore} with respect to each physician's held-out gold responses. We also report validation-set perplexity (PPL) as a measure of adaptation quality.
(2)~Human evaluation: three board-certified physicians rate 100 randomly sampled test queries each on 5-point Likert scales across three dimensions---\emph{Personalization} (stylistic and terminological fidelity to the target physician), \emph{Clinical Accuracy} (medical correctness and appropriateness), and \emph{Fluency} (grammaticality and naturalness)---plus blind A/B preference judgments. Inter-rater agreement reaches Fleiss' $\kappa=0.72$ for personalization and $\kappa=0.68$ for clinical accuracy (substantial agreement).
(3)~Deployment efficiency: per-adapter storage footprint (MB), adapter loading latency (ms), and end-to-end inference latency (seconds per response). For merged-adapter deployments, we additionally report the \emph{Retention Rate}---the ratio of the merged adapter's BLEU-4 to that of the individual LoRA-Single adapter, expressed as a percentage.

\subsection{Results}

\subsubsection{Automatic Evaluation}~\\
Table~\ref{tab:accuracy} reports the automatic evaluation results across all methods and model scales.
The Base Model achieves BLEU-4 scores of 3.2/5.1/7.5 at 1B/3B/7B, confirming that zero-shot prompting cannot produce physician-specific outputs even as model scale increases.
Shared LoRA improves to 5.1/7.8/11.3, demonstrating that general clinical adaptation is beneficial, but the lack of per-physician differentiation leaves a substantial gap relative to individual adaptation at every scale.

Among the merging baselines, simple averaging (LoRA-Merge-Avg) performs worst across all scales (BLEU-4 3.7/5.8/9.1), with retention rates degrading sharply for smaller models (53.6\% at 1B vs.\ 64.1\% at 7B), indicating that smaller models are more vulnerable to parameter interference.
Task arithmetic (LoRA-Merge-TA) improves retention to 81.2/83.7/84.5\%, but still loses 15--19\% of individual performance.
The original TIES-MERGING achieves 97.1--97.2\% retention across scales (BLEU-4 6.7/10.1/13.8), confirming that explicit sign resolution is effective regardless of model capacity.
Our Sign-Consistent Fusion (SCF) further improves upon TIES-MERGING, matching the LoRA-Single upper bound across all metrics and scales (BLEU-4 6.9/10.4/14.2, retention 100\%).
The full WebDoctor system, which combines SCF with identity-based DAR routing and physician-specific prompt prefixes, preserves this performance, demonstrating that the merged deployment mode introduces no measurable personalization degradation.

\subsubsection{Human Evaluation}~\\
Table~\ref{tab:ttft} presents the human evaluation results across model scales.
The personalization gap between the Base Model and WebDoctor widens with model scale: from 1.4$\rightarrow$3.3 at 1B (+1.9 points) to 2.8$\rightarrow$4.5 at 7B (+1.7 points), suggesting that larger models have more expressive capacity to absorb physician-specific stylistic patterns.
The Base Model receives a preference share of only 3--8\%, corroborating the automatic findings: physicians do not perceive generic outputs as matching their communication style at any scale.
Shared LoRA achieves moderate improvement (preference 8--15\%), but still falls well below acceptable levels.
The LoRA-Merge-TIES variant (personalization 3.1/4.0/4.3) demonstrates that merged-adapter deployment can achieve respectable personalization, though individual adapters retain a lead.
The full WebDoctor system achieves the highest ratings across all dimensions, with personalization reaching 4.5/5.0 at 7B and physicians preferring its outputs in 35--48\% of blind A/B comparisons.
Notably, clinical accuracy is consistently high across all adapted methods (3.4--4.5), confirming that personalization does not compromise medical correctness; the primary differentiation occurs along the personalization dimension.

\subsubsection{DAR Routing Accuracy}~\\
We evaluate the DAR router's query-based retrieval accuracy on the test set.
Since DAR operates on fixed-size physician-style embeddings (384-dim Sentence-BERT), routing accuracy is independent of the underlying generation model scale.
With $m=1$, the router achieves a top-1 accuracy of 82.4\%, correctly identifying the target physician from the patient query alone in over four-fifths of cases.
Accuracy rises to 94.7\% at top-3 and 98.1\% at top-5, indicating that even when the exact physician is not the top-ranked candidate, the correct adapter is reliably retrieved within a small candidate set.
Random routing (uniform selection among $K=120$ physicians) achieves only 0.8\% top-1 accuracy, confirming that the DAR embeddings encode meaningful physician-style discriminative information.
The routing overhead is negligible (4.8\,ms per query), well within the real-time latency budget of internet hospital workflows ($<5$\,ms target).


\subsection{Component Validation and Ablation Study}

\subsubsection{LoRA Rank and Data Efficiency}~\\
We vary the LoRA rank $r \in \{4, 8, 16, 32\}$ to assess the impact of adapter capacity on personalization quality across model scales.
Table~\ref{tab:ablation} (top block) shows that $r=8$ offers the best accuracy-efficiency trade-off at all scales: $r=4$ achieves BLEU-4 4.9/9.5/13.4 with a 4.2\,MB adapter footprint, while $r=8$ reaches 5.4/10.4/14.2 with 6.8\,MB, and $r=16$ yields only a marginal gain (5.5/10.5/14.3) at nearly double the storage cost (13.1\,MB).
$r=32$ provides no further improvement (5.5/10.5/14.3), suggesting that the personalization signal saturates at moderate rank regardless of base model capacity.
This is consistent with the limited per-physician training data (median 186 dialogues): higher-rank adapters overfit without additional regularization.

We further examine the relationship between per-physician training set size and BLEU-4.
Physicians in the top quartile by dialogue count ($\ge 287$ dialogues) outperform those in the bottom quartile ($\le 98$ dialogues) by 1.3--1.5 BLEU-4 points across model scales, confirming that personalization quality benefits from larger individual corpora.
However, even the bottom-quartile physicians achieve a BLEU-4 more than double that of the corresponding Base Model at every scale, demonstrating the data efficiency of LoRA-based adaptation.

\subsubsection{SCF Performance Preservation}~\\
Table~\ref{tab:scf_retention} reports the retention rate of SCF as a function of the trim fraction $\tau$ and the number of merged adapters $K$, evaluated on the 3B model.
At the optimal $\tau=0.20$ and the full physician pool ($K=120$), SCF retains 100\% of individual adapter performance (BLEU-4 10.4 vs.\ 10.4), outperforming the original TIES-MERGING (97.1\%) at the same $\tau$.
Retention is robust across a range of $\tau$ values: $\tau=0.15$ achieves 98.4\%, $\tau=0.25$ achieves 99.2\%, and only at extremes ($\tau\le0.10$ or $\tau\ge0.40$) does retention drop below 95\%.
Scaling $K$ from 20 to 120 physicians, the SCF retention rate degrades gracefully from 100\% to 99.7\%, indicating that the enhanced sign-consistency mechanism effectively resolves sign conflicts even as the adapter pool grows.
In contrast, simple averaging degrades sharply from 87.3\% at $K=20$ to 55.8\% at $K=120$, confirming that interference accumulates multiplicatively without explicit sign resolution.

\textbf{Deployment Efficiency.} Each individual LoRA adapter requires 6.8\,MB of storage and approximately 120\,ms to load from disk.
For $K=120$ physicians, per-adapter serving would require 816\,MB of aggregate storage and, in the worst case, 120\,ms of loading latency per distinct physician request.
The merged SCF adapter occupies 6.8\,MB (equivalent to a single adapter) and is loaded once at server startup, eliminating per-request adapter switching overhead.
End-to-end inference latency with the merged adapter is 0.9/1.8/3.2\,s per response (1B/3B/7B) on a single H20-3e GPU, identical to the individual adapter case, confirming that SCF introduces no computational overhead at inference time.

\subsubsection{Ablation Study}~\\
We ablate the key design choices of WebDoctor, reporting BLEU-4 and human-evaluated personalization scores (5-point scale) across all three model scales.
Table~\ref{tab:ablation} (bottom block) presents the results.
Removing the DAR router and relying solely on a generic prompt prefix (``w/o DAR'') causes a moderate drop across scales (BLEU-4 6.4/10.2/13.9, personalization 3.0/4.0/4.4), confirming that explicit physician identity conditioning via adaptive routing is important for stylistic fidelity.
Disabling SCF and serving individual adapters (``w/o SCF'') preserves full personalization quality but at the cost of $K$-fold storage and loading overhead, as discussed above.
Removing both DAR and SCF (i.e., Shared LoRA) yields the largest degradation (BLEU-4 5.1/7.8/11.3, personalization 1.9/2.8/3.4), confirming that both components contribute independently and additively to personalization quality.
Varying the SCF trim fraction confirms the optimal $\tau=0.20$: $\tau=0.10$ is overly aggressive (BLEU-4 5.8/9.3/13.1, retention 84.1/89.4/92.3\%), while $\tau=0.30$ retains more parameters but introduces noise (BLEU-4 6.1/9.5/13.4).
